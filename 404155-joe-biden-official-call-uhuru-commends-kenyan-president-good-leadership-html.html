Joe Biden makes first official call to Uhuru, commends Kenyan president for good leadership

Posted by Almeda Bohannan on Monday, August 12, 2024

- US president Joe Biden lauded Kenya's effort in fighting terrorism and restoring peace in the Horn of Africa

- He affirmed America's commitment to building strong bilateral relations with Nairobi to foster economic prosperity

- Biden also promised to support Uhuru's administration in dealing with the security concern in the region and foster peaceful co-existence

President of the United States (US) Joe Biden has applauded his Kenyan counterpart Uhuru Kenyatta for the good leadership he has demonstrated in the Horn of Africa.

Biden made his first official call to Uhuru on Thursday, February 25, night, and assured him of America's support in dealing with security and other related challenges affecting the region.

Strong bilateral ties

The US president also affirmed his administration's commitment to building strong bilateral relations with Kenya to foster economic prosperity,

“The president (Biden) applauded Kenya’s leadership in the Horn of Africa and commitment to counter-terrorism, economic growth, addressing climate change, and sustainable development,” the White House said in a brief statement.

The two leaders also discussed the deteriorating humanitarian and human rights crises in Ethiopia’s Tigray region and the need to prevent further loss of life.

Kenya's role in Somalia

Kenya has been playing a key role in restoring peace and stability in Somalia and, recently, Ethiopia.

In 2011, Kenya deployed its military personnel to Mogadishu to restore calm in the war-torn country. The soldiers are still keeping peace under the African Union Mission in Somalia (AMISOM).

Kenya has also been home to thousands of refugees from Somalia and some from Ethiopia.

Despite engaging in an unending diplomatic tiff with Somalia, Nairobi has remained a key partner and worthy neighbour to President Mohamed Farmajo-led country.

Uhuru's role in Ethiopia

In November 2020, Uhuru held talks with Ethiopia's deputy prime minister Demeke Mokonnen at State House in Nairobi and urged the warring parties to find peaceful means of ending the feud.

The Kenyan president urged Ethiopia's federal government and the Tigrayan People's Liberation Front (TPLF) to de-escalate the conflict to pave the way for dialogue.

"While acknowledging the internal efforts being made to end the conflict, President Kenyatta urged the warring parties to prioritise humanitarian needs of local populations by opening up corridors for essential supplies," read a statement by State House.

Escalating unrest

Tension has remained high in Ethiopia as the war pitting government troops against civilian forces fighting under TPLF continues to escalate.

The situation got worse when Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed unleashed national defence forces on TPLF fighters on November 4, following the latter's alleged attack on a federal military base in Ahmara region.

On Friday, November 13, two missiles believed to have been unleashed by TPLF targeted two airports.

One hit an airport in Gondar and left it partially damaged, while another one fired simultaneously landed just outside of the airport at Bahir Dar.

Biden promised to work closely with Kenya to de-escalate the civil unrest in Abiy Ahmed-led country and further provide humanitarian services to affected groups.
